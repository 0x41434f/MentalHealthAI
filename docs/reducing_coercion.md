In my experience working as a psychiatric technician and mental health worker, I have been exposed daily to the complex reality of mental health care, where the pursuit of patient safety often intersects, and sometimes clashes, with legal rights and fundamental dignity. Stepping into a locked psychiatric facility grounds you in a profound reality: you are standing where human struggle meets strict, life-altering legal decisions. My professional life is framed by detailed regulations, yet it is profoundly motivated by the human goal of reducing coercion and trauma in vulnerable populations. This duality is particularly evident in California, where civil commitment laws have historically received a low national grade of D- for imposing barriers to receiving timely treatment.

The legal mechanism for involuntary commitment in California is the **Lanterman-Petris-Short (LPS) Act**, which dictates how an adult can be held when they cannot or will not accept voluntary help. This intervention begins with an emergency **72-hour hold (WIC 5150)** for evaluation and crisis intervention, which is **not a criminal arrest**. This severe restriction of liberty is permitted only if the person, as a result of a mental health disorder, poses one of three criteria: they are a danger to **others**, a danger to **themselves**, or they are **gravely disabled**. The definition of Grave Disability is crucial, meaning a person is incapable of providing for basic needs like food, clothing, shelter, or safety. With the passage of **Senate Bill 43 (SB 43)**, this definition expanded to explicitly include severe substance use disorder as a basis for finding a person gravely disabled. When deciding on this hold, staff must consider all available relevant information, including the **historical course** of the person’s mental disorder. Importantly, a person is generally **not gravely disabled** if they can survive safely with the help of a willing and able relative, friend, or other third party; however, that third party must explicitly indicate their willingness and ability to help in writing.

If the person remains acutely unwell after the 72-hour hold, they may be certified for a **14-day intensive treatment hold (WIC 5250)**. At this stage, legal due process requires a **certification review hearing** to determine if **probable cause** exists to continue the detention. Patients must be informed of their right to challenge the commitment and receive help from an independent **Patients’ Rights Advocate (PRA)**, who only represents the patient's interests. It is essential for readers to know that while advocating against detention, the mere act of expressing a desire to **refuse treatment is not enough to prove** a need for commitment, though this objection should be stated openly. For prolonged illness, the most restrictive measure is the **LPS Conservatorship (WIC 5000)**, where a conservator is appointed by the court to make major life decisions regarding treatment and placement.

This journey through involuntary legal status heightens the ethical mandate to minimize harm, particularly concerning **seclusion and restraint (S/R)**, the extreme measures used to isolate or physically restrict a person. As peers who have experienced psychiatric facilities know, being restrained or secluded is a **frightening, traumatic, and degrading experience**. S/R is strictly regulated by law, which states it can **only be used to ensure immediate physical safety** when **less restrictive interventions** have been determined to be ineffective. Despite this clear legal boundary, the unfortunate reality is that S/R is **used mostly for loud, disruptive, or non-compliant behavior**, rather than exclusively for immediate serious harm. This misuse is concerning because restraints carry the risk of severe injuries, deaths, and enduring **psychological trauma**.

My focus, and the goal of humane practice, is to reduce and eliminate these coercive interventions by making **crisis prevention a team effort** shared between staff and patients. This means embracing strategies rooted in dignity and collaboration:

First, staff must learn the patient's **unique "triggers"** and **early warning signs**, which might include internal, non-obvious symptoms like **headaches** or emotional distress triggered by noise, lack of privacy, or certain tones of voice. We must recognize that due to a **history of trauma**, the use of S/R may cause a person to **re-live the traumatic event**. Therefore, treatment must prioritize ensuring **physical and psychological safety**.

Second, we must prioritize **de-escalation** techniques. This involves anticipating distress and allowing patients to use individual coping strategies, such as **pacing back and forth to lessen their anxiety**, rather than immediately trying to stop or control their movement. We know that common staff behaviors that are **not helpful** when someone is upset include not being listened to, being ignored, or being told to sit down.

Third, planning must be proactive. Patients should be assisted in creating an **Advance Directive for Health Care**, a document that specifies their wishes regarding seclusion and restraint or alternatives, such as preferring to be restrained by holding hands or by a person of a specific gender. The care environment must also support recovery; restricting, outdated physical environments should be replaced with spaces that allow patients to regulate themselves, such as **accessible sensory areas** designed to help them escape overstimulation.

Finally, accountability is crucial. Because prone physical restraints are particularly dangerous and should be deliberately avoided whenever possible, if prone containment is used in an emergency, a staff member must **observe the person for any signs of physical duress** throughout the use. After any S/R incident, **California law requires a debriefing session** to discuss what led up to the event, suggest safer methods for the future, and formally **modify the crisis plan**.

By focusing on prevention, individual advocacy, and rigorous adherence to the least restrictive care principles, we can move the mental health system from simply reacting to crisis toward genuinely supporting recovery and upholding the rights of every individual.
